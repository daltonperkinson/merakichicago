Human Agency Index (HAI)

Project Plan & Research Foundation — Working Draft, April 2026

What is this document? This is the working project plan for an academic paper proposing the Human Agency Index (HAI) — a multi-dimensional scoring framework for measuring whether meaningful human control actually exists in any AI-enabled system. The dimensions were derived inductively from 29 documented cases of AI-related harm, not asserted top-down.

3. The Seven HAI Dimensions

The failure mode analysis reveals seven recurring, independently identifiable types of human oversight failure. Each appeared in at least 18 of 29 cases. Six of the seven were identified in initial conceptual work; the seventh — Affected Party Awareness — emerged directly from the case evidence.

#1 Genuine Engagement 25 of 29 cases (86%)

What it measures: Does the system require genuine human cognitive engagement — not just a button press, a rubber stamp, or a nominal “review” — at meaningful intervals in the decision process?

Why it ranks #1: The single most common failure mode. In case after case, a human was technically “in the loop” but not genuinely engaged. If you could only measure one thing about an AI system’s human oversight, this would be it — because every other dimension is moot if nobody is actually paying attention.

Key Cases

UnitedHealth nH Predict: Clinical staff pressured to keep stays within 1% of AI prediction — transforming “human review” into rubber-stamping. Consequence: patients died.
Michigan MiDAS: Human fraud examiners eliminated to cut costs. 93% error rate. Consequence: 40,000 false fraud accusations, 11,000+ bankruptcies.
Uber AV — Herzberg: Backup safety driver was watching Hulu for 42 minutes before fatal collision. No system monitored whether the monitor was monitoring. Consequence: first autonomous vehicle pedestrian death.
Tesla Autopilot: Drivers could use the system for extended periods with hands off wheel and eyes off road. Consequence: 65+ deaths.
Netherlands Childcare Benefits: 26,000 cases processed at volume without individual human assessment. Consequence: suicides, homelessness, government resigned.

#2 Reversibility 23 of 29 cases (79%)

What it measures: After the AI has acted or produced a decision, can a human effectively undo, reverse, or remedy the outcome? How much time exists between AI action and irreversible consequence?

Why it ranks #2: Severity scales directly with irreversibility. When AI actions cannot be undone — a patient dies, a person spends months in jail, a pedestrian is struck — no amount of accountability or transparency after the fact helps the victim.

Key Cases

Australia Robodebt: 400,000 people received automated debt demands. Even after the scheme was found unlawful, the suicides could not be reversed. Consequence: A$1.87 billion settlement cannot restore lives.
Uber AV / Tesla / Cruise: Vehicle strikes are irreversible within milliseconds. Uber had disabled the emergency braking. Consequence: 65+ deaths across these systems.
Character.ai: Two teenagers died. No crisis escalation existed. Consequence: two children dead.
Clearview AI — Lipps: Five months of wrongful imprisonment. Consequence: 5+ months stolen from a grandmother’s life.

#3 Decision Authority 22 of 29 cases (76%)

What it measures: Who actually makes the consequential decision — the human or the AI? Not who is supposed to make it, but who functionally makes it in practice.

Why it ranks #3: The most intuitive dimension, but ranked below Engagement and Reversibility because nominal authority without engagement is meaningless, and authority without reversibility is reckless.

Key Cases

UnitedHealth nH Predict: Designed authority: clinical staff. Actual authority: AI predicts, employees pressured to stay within 1%. The AI decided; the human signed. Consequence: patients died.
COMPAS Sentencing: Judges routinely followed scores with minimal scrutiny of the proprietary methodology. Consequence: Black defendants received longer sentences based on 61%-accurate algorithm.
All facial recognition cases: FR match treated as sufficient for arrest warrants — no corroborating investigation. Consequence: wrongful arrests, months of imprisonment.

#4 Transparency 22 of 29 cases (76%)

What it measures: Does the human decision-maker have sufficient information about how the AI reached its output, what data it used, what its known limitations are, and what confidence level should be assigned to its recommendation?

Key Cases

COMPAS Sentencing: Proprietary algorithm. Judges told a score; couldn’t examine methodology, weighting, or error rates. Consequence: Black defendants 77% more likely flagged high-risk.
Optum Risk Algorithm: Clinicians didn’t know algorithm used spending-as-proxy-for-health. Consequence: Black patients received 50% less recommended care.
Tesla Autopilot: “Full Self-Driving” marketing created false impression of capability. Consequence: 65+ fatalities where drivers over-trusted the system.

#5 Affected Party Awareness 21 of 29 cases (72%)

What it measures: Does the person affected by the AI’s decision know that an AI system was involved? Do they have enough information to understand the basis for the decision and challenge it if warranted?

This dimension is new. It was not in the original six — it emerged directly from the case evidence. In 21 of 29 cases, the subject of the decision had no idea an algorithm was involved. This is distinct from operator Transparency and from Intervention Accessibility. A person cannot use an override mechanism they don’t know they need.

Key Cases

Netherlands Childcare: 26,000 families accused of fraud by an algorithm that used nationality as a risk factor. Not told an algorithm made the determination. Consequence: suicides, homelessness; the government resigned.
SafeRent Tenant Screening: Only 3% of denied renters knew the name of the screening company. Consequence: systemic housing discrimination.
Sweden Welfare: Affected individuals had no way to learn they were assessed. System operated 11 years before journalists exposed it. Consequence: decade of undetected discrimination.
All facial recognition arrest cases: Defendants not told FR was the basis. Warrants said “credible source.” Consequence: inability to mount a meaningful defense.

#6 Intervention Accessibility 19 of 29 cases (66%)

What it measures: How easy is it for a human to stop, redirect, override, or correct the AI mid-process? This includes both technical accessibility (is there a mechanism?) and practical accessibility (can a real person actually use it?).

Key distinction from Reversibility: Reversibility measures whether harm can be undone after it occurs. Intervention Accessibility measures whether a human can prevent the harm before it occurs.

Key Cases

UnitedHealth nH Predict: Appeals existed on paper but were described by the federal judge as “futile.” Consequence: patients died waiting for the process to work.
Uber AV: Emergency braking was deliberately disabled. Consequence: pedestrian death.
Character.ai: No crisis escalation pathway. When a teenager expressed suicidal ideation, no mechanism to alert anyone. Consequence: teen suicides.

#7 Accountability Traceability 18 of 29 cases (62%)

What it measures: Can you identify a specific human (or role) who is responsible for a given AI-driven outcome? Is the chain of responsibility documented, auditable, and enforceable?

Why it ranks #7: Essential for deterrence and remediation, but primarily a retrospective dimension — it matters most after harm has occurred. The other six are preventive.

Key Cases

Cruise Robotaxi: Submitted falsified reports to regulators, omitting the dragging. Accountability chain actively destroyed. Consequence: $500K criminal fine for obstruction.
Clearview AI — Reid: Detective wrote “credible source” instead of disclosing facial recognition. Broke the accountability chain at every subsequent stage. Consequence: wrongful arrest; concealment prevented oversight.
Tesla Autopilot: Marketing implied autonomy; terms of service required attention. Deliberate accountability gap. Consequence: 65+ deaths with unresolved accountability.

5Affected Party Awareness21 (72%)The “last mile” — protects the subject, not just the operator. NEW dimension.

The Dependency Chain

Each dimension is only effective if the dimensions above it are satisfied. This structure provides a principled basis for weighting the composite score:

1. Genuine Engagement → Is anyone actually watching?
2. Reversibility → If something goes wrong, can it be undone?
3. Decision Authority → Who actually decides?
4. Transparency → Does the decider have enough information?
5. Affected Party Awareness → Does the affected person know?
6. Intervention Accessibility → Can someone stop or redirect?
7. Accountability Traceability → Can you identify who’s responsible?

6. The Open Question: Scoring Model Options

The seven dimensions and their ranking are empirically grounded. The open question — and the area where mathematical expertise is essential — is: how do we turn seven per-dimension scores into a defensible composite index?

Below are three candidate approaches. Each has different mathematical properties, different strengths, and different trade-offs. The right answer likely involves elements of more than one. The goal of this section is to frame the question precisely enough that a mathematician can evaluate the options, identify weaknesses, and propose a rigorous formalization.

Option A: Weighted Linear Composite

Approach: Each dimension is scored on a defined scale (e.g., 1–5). Weights are derived from the dependency chain — Genuine Engagement weighted highest, Accountability Traceability weighted lowest. The composite is a weighted average, mapped to tier classifications.

HAI = Σ(wi × si) / Σ(wi)

Example weights: GE=7, RE=6, DA=5, TR=4, APA=3, IA=2, AT=1
Score range: 1.0 – 5.0 → mapped to tiers (e.g., A/B/C/D/F or HAI-1 through HAI-5)

Strengths: Simple, intuitive, easy to communicate. Familiar to anyone who has worked with credit ratings or LEED scores. Weighting reflects the empirically derived dependency chain.

Weaknesses: A high score on one dimension can compensate for a catastrophically low score on another. For example, a system with perfect Accountability Traceability but zero Genuine Engagement could still produce a moderate composite — but the case evidence shows such a system is dangerous. Linear models assume dimensional independence, which the dependency chain explicitly contradicts.

Questions for Mathematical Review

Are the proposed weights defensible, or should they be derived differently (e.g., from case frequency, from principal component analysis of the failure matrix, from expert elicitation)? How sensitive is the composite to weight selection? Can we demonstrate that the ranking is robust under reasonable weight perturbations?

Option B: Minimum-Gated Model (“Weakest Link”)

Approach: Each dimension is scored 1–5. The composite score is gated by the lowest-scoring dimension: the overall HAI cannot exceed the minimum score by more than one tier. This captures the dependency chain directly — a system cannot achieve high overall human agency if any single dimension is critically deficient.

HAI = min(mean(s1...s7), min(si) + 1)

Example: scores = [5, 5, 5, 5, 5, 5, 1]
Mean = 4.43, but min + 1 = 2
HAI = 2 (gated by the weakest dimension)

Strengths: Directly encodes the insight from the case evidence: a single critical failure in any dimension can make the entire system dangerous. UnitedHealth had nominal human authority but zero genuine engagement — a gated model would correctly flag this as high-risk. Prevents “averaging away” catastrophic gaps.

Weaknesses: May be overly punitive for systems with one moderately low score. The “+1” threshold is arbitrary — what is the principled basis for the gate width? Might discourage investment in dimensions other than the weakest (if improving a strong dimension cannot raise the composite).

Questions for Mathematical Review

What is the right gating function? Is +1 too generous, too restrictive? Could the gate be dimension-specific (e.g., Genuine Engagement and Reversibility gate more tightly than Accountability Traceability)? Is there a mathematical formulation that captures both the weakest-link property and the weighted importance of dimensions?

Option C: Hybrid — Weighted Composite with Mandatory Minimums

Approach: Combines elements of Options A and B. The composite uses a weighted average (Option A) but the tier classification imposes floor requirements. To achieve a given HAI tier, every dimension must meet a minimum threshold for that tier — similar to how LEED certification requires points across categories, not just total points.

Composite = Σ(wi × si) / Σ(wi)

Tier requirements:
  HAI-A (Excellent): Composite ≥ 4.0 AND all dimensions ≥ 3
  HAI-B (Adequate): Composite ≥ 3.0 AND all dimensions ≥ 2
  HAI-C (Deficient): Composite ≥ 2.0 AND no dimension = 1
  HAI-D (Critical): Any dimension = 1
  HAI-F (Absent): Three or more dimensions = 1

Strengths: Preserves the information value of the weighted composite while preventing dangerous gaps from being hidden. The tier classification is intuitive for policymakers (similar to bond ratings or food safety grades). Incentivizes improvement across all dimensions, not just the weakest.

Weaknesses: More complex to communicate. The floor thresholds for each tier need principled justification. The system has more degrees of freedom (weights + thresholds), which means more parameters to calibrate and defend.

Questions for Mathematical Review

Can we derive the floor thresholds empirically from the case bank (e.g., what minimum scores would have flagged systems that caused documented harm)? Is there a way to mathematically prove that the tier classification is consistent (i.e., that improving any dimension score never worsens the tier)? Should the top two dimensions (Genuine Engagement, Reversibility) have stricter floor requirements than the bottom two?

What we need from a mathematical co-author

The research, case bank, failure mode analysis, dimension derivation, and ranking are complete. The lit review and practitioner validation can be handled independently. The critical gap is the mathematical formalization of the scoring model. Specifically:

1. Scale definition: What scoring scale for each dimension (1–5? 0–10? continuous?) and what anchors define each level?

2. Composite function: Which of the three options above (or a fourth alternative) produces the most defensible composite?

3. Mathematical properties: Independence testing — are the seven dimensions actually measuring distinct constructs? Sensitivity analysis — how robust is the composite to scoring noise? Boundary conditions — does the model behave sensibly at extremes?

4. Validation: Score 6–8 real-world systems from the case bank using the formalized model. Demonstrate that HAI differentiates systems that existing single-axis frameworks (Knight, SAE) cannot.
